\begin{abstract}
We study video object removal and background restoration for dynamic scenes.
Our current pipeline includes a classical baseline based on object segmentation, sparse optical flow, temporal median borrowing, and \texttt{cv2.inpaint}, followed by a stronger video inpainting backend using ProPainter.
Rather than changing every component at once, we first keep the removal masks fixed and replace only the restoration stage, which gives a controlled comparison between classical and modern video completion methods.
Our current results on \texttt{bmx-trees}, \texttt{tennis}, and two self-recorded wild videos show that ProPainter substantially improves visual quality over the baseline restoration module, while remaining limited by the quality of the upstream masks.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Video object removal aims to erase dynamic foreground objects while recovering a temporally consistent background.
This task is useful for scene cleanup, editing, robotics, and video understanding.
In this project, we compare a controllable classical baseline with a modern AI-driven pipeline, then analyze failure cases caused by weak automatic masks.
Our near-term goal is not only to produce a working baseline, but to build a clean experimental story: keep the removal region fixed, replace the restoration backend, and measure or visualize the resulting quality gap.

\subsection{Part 3: Failure-case Extension}
The current most informative failure case is \texttt{wild\_video2}, where the moving person is not consistently covered by the automatic mask generator.
This means the failure is not caused by the inpainting backend itself; the object is simply never marked for removal in several frames.
The most direct extension is therefore a targeted mask-improvement experiment, optionally followed by keyframe-level generative repair if the hidden background is never sufficiently observed.

\subsection{Qualitative Results}
The strongest current qualitative comparisons come from \texttt{bmx-trees} and \texttt{tennis}.
In both cases, keeping the masks fixed and replacing the restoration backend with ProPainter significantly reduces blocky artifacts, transparent ghosting, and inconsistent texture synthesis.
\texttt{wild\_video1} serves as a real-scene success case, while \texttt{wild\_video2} highlights the sensitivity of the overall pipeline to mask completeness.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}
The most important current failure mode is weak mask generation.
On \texttt{wild\_video2}, the moving person is only partially captured by the baseline mask generator, so both the classical baseline and ProPainter fail to remove the target in missed frames.
This makes \texttt{wild\_video2} an effective targeted case for prompt-guided SAM~2 refinement.
Additional failure modes include overly large masks on thin structures in \texttt{bmx-trees} and residual transparency artifacts in the Part 1 restoration output.

\section{Conclusion}
Our current experiments already support a clear intermediate conclusion: when the removal mask is held fixed, ProPainter provides substantially better restoration quality than the classical temporal median plus \texttt{cv2.inpaint} baseline.
The main remaining bottleneck is mask quality rather than inpainting quality, which motivates a targeted next-step experiment using stronger video mask propagation on \texttt{wild\_video2}.
